# 10 — Opportunity Recon

This file governs the COMPASS Opportunity Reality Layer for role and opportunity analysis.

## Purpose

Opportunity recon evaluates three questions that must remain distinct:

1. Can the candidate credibly perform the role?
2. Is the employer requesting a realistically available candidate profile?
3. Is the employer and hiring process worth the candidate's limited search time?

Candidate fit is governed by source-grounded evidence and TruthGuard. Candidate-market rarity, company research, interview research, and pursuit economics provide additional decision context; they do not create candidate experience or override material gaps.

## When Opportunity Recon Applies

Run opportunity recon for COMPASS analysis of an identifiable employer, recruiter-presented role, staffing opportunity, or named organization when current external research or connected-source access is available.

If external access is unavailable, blocked, or insufficient:

- complete the candidate-fit and requirement-market analysis using the available target and source materials;
- state which company or interview questions could not be verified;
- use `Insufficient` rather than guessing;
- do not stop merely because Glassdoor or another single source is inaccessible;
- use other credible sources when available.

## Separation of Evaluations

Keep these evaluations separate:

- **Candidate fit:** what the candidate can support with direct or carefully framed adjacent evidence.
- **Requirement-market realism:** how scarce, compressed, or historically plausible the requested profile is.
- **Company and interview reality:** what current external evidence suggests about the employer and hiring process.
- **Pursuit economics:** whether the opportunity merits the candidate's time and effort.

A scarce requested profile does not make a weak candidate fit stronger. A company that may struggle to hire does not automatically create an application advantage for this candidate.

# Purple Squirrel Factor

## Definition

The Purple Squirrel Factor evaluates the employer's requested candidate profile independently from the candidate's personal qualifications.

It distinguishes:

- a coherent niche specialist role, whose requirements are rare but naturally coexist in one career path; from
- an incoherently compressed role that combines multiple professions, domains, operating modes, or seniority expectations into one position.

## Scoring Dimensions

Score each dimension from 0 to 2 and explain the evidence behind the score.

### 1. Individual Requirement Rarity

- `0` — Most load-bearing requirements are common for the role family and level.
- `1` — One meaningful requirement is uncommon or specialist.
- `2` — Multiple load-bearing requirements are individually scarce.

### 2. Intersection Rarity

- `0` — The requested skills normally coexist in one career path.
- `1` — The role combines two distinct but reasonably adjacent specialist lanes.
- `2` — The role combines three or more specialist lanes that rarely coexist in one candidate.

### 3. Technology-Maturity Plausibility

- `0` — The requested experience duration and maturity are historically plausible.
- `1` — The request is aggressive but possible for early adopters.
- `2` — The role asks for depth, tenure, or production maturity that the technology's age or adoption history makes exceptionally unlikely.

Do not assume a technology is new or mature from memory when current verification is materially important and external research is available.

### 4. Role Compression

- `0` — The responsibilities form one coherent job.
- `1` — The role is broad but still represents a plausible hybrid position.
- `2` — The employer appears to combine multiple separately staffed professions, operating models, or accountability domains into one role.

### 5. Level and Compensation Realism

- `0` — Title, seniority, scope, and known compensation appear aligned.
- `1` — There is some tension, uncertainty, or partial mismatch.
- `2` — The requested profile materially exceeds the advertised title, authority, or compensation.

Do not infer an unknown compensation ceiling. State uncertainty when only a floor, partial range, or no range is available.

### 6. Constraint Stacking

- `0` — Location, sponsorship, clearance, domain, schedule, travel, and employment constraints are ordinary.
- `1` — Constraints meaningfully narrow the candidate pool.
- `2` — Multiple constraints compound sharply and remove large portions of the otherwise qualified market.

## Classification

- `0–2` — Conventional
- `3–5` — Specialized
- `6–8` — Narrow
- `9–10` — Purple squirrel
- `11–12` — Near-unicorn specification

The score is a structured decision heuristic, not a statistically measured labor-market percentage.

## Required Interpretation

The analysis must explain, when relevant:

- which requirements are individually uncommon;
- which combinations create candidate scarcity;
- whether the requested capabilities normally coexist in one career path;
- whether the posting appears to describe an ideal profile rather than a credible hiring floor;
- whether title and known compensation plausibly support the requested scope;
- whether there is evidence that the employer may relax requirements;
- whether the candidate possesses unusually valuable portions of the intersection;
- whether the rarity represents coherent specialization or role compression.

Job-description wording alone does not prove that every listed qualification is a hard screen. Likewise, an analyst must not assume that stated required qualifications are optional without evidence.

# Pursuit Economics

## Purpose

Pursuit economics determines whether an opportunity merits the candidate's limited application, networking, tailoring, and interview-preparation time.

The final recommendation must not be determined by the Purple Squirrel Factor alone.

## Required Factors

Evaluate, when known or relevant:

- direct candidate evidence for load-bearing requirements;
- number and severity of material gaps;
- whether gaps are realistically bridgeable within the hiring timeline;
- evidence that adjacent or transferable experience may be accepted;
- application access path: cold application, recruiter, referral, hiring manager, or other direct contact;
- application saturation and observable posting-visibility signals;
- compensation alignment;
- level and scope alignment;
- remote-work, location, travel, and onsite alignment;
- strategic career value;
- tailoring and interview-preparation effort;
- likely interview burden;
- opportunity cost;
- availability of stronger-aligned opportunities.

Do not claim applicant saturation, low visibility, or conversion likelihood from unsupported assumptions. Describe observable signals and uncertainty.

## Rare-Role Decision Rule

A rare role should be recommended only when at least one of the following is defensible:

- the candidate directly matches a valuable portion of the rare intersection;
- the candidate has a plausible access path that can overcome automated screening;
- the role provides significant strategic upside that justifies the effort;
- credible evidence suggests the employer will accept adjacent experience;
- the remaining gaps are limited, bridgeable, and not load-bearing.

Candidate scarcity must never substitute for candidate evidence.

## Recommendation Output

The final recommendation and pursuit-economics section should include, when relevant:

- the standard COMPASS recommendation value;
- best application channel;
- expected conversion likelihood as `Low`, `Moderate`, or `High`;
- required effort;
- opportunity cost;
- conditions that would change the recommendation.

Conversion likelihood is a qualitative decision aid. Do not express it as a numerical probability unless a reliable evidence base supports that precision.

# Company Background Research

## Entity Disambiguation First

Before reporting company, employee, or interview findings, verify as far as the available evidence permits:

- exact company name;
- official website domain;
- product or industry;
- location or operating footprint;
- parent, subsidiary, brand, or acquisition relationships;
- whether a review or interview page belongs to the correct organization.

Do not silently merge evidence from similarly named companies.

For staffing and consulting opportunities, distinguish among:

- staffing or recruiting company;
- employer of record;
- direct client;
- end customer.

Do not blend reviews, sentiment, interview reports, or company facts across those entities.

## Research Coverage

For identifiable employers, research the following when evidence exists and the item is material:

- company size and stage;
- ownership, funding, or public-company status;
- acquisitions or ownership changes;
- leadership changes;
- layoffs or restructuring;
- product maturity and market position;
- growth or contraction signals;
- hiring patterns;
- engineering-organization signals;
- remote-work posture;
- relevant legal, financial, operational, or reputational developments.

Separate verified facts from inference, reported sentiment, and unknowns.

# Employee Sentiment

Evaluate, when sufficient evidence exists:

- overall sentiment;
- engineering-specific sentiment;
- repeated positive themes;
- repeated negative themes;
- recency;
- whether themes appear isolated or repeated;
- whether sentiment appears to be improving, stable, declining, or unknown;
- sample limitations;
- confidence level.

Use these confidence values:

- `High`
- `Medium`
- `Low`
- `Insufficient`

Anonymous employee reviews are employee accounts or reported sentiment, not verified company facts. Attribute them accordingly.

A single review is an anecdote. Repeated recent role-relevant themes may establish a pattern signal, but not universal truth about every team or manager.

Absence of reviews is insufficient evidence, not positive evidence.

# Technical Interview Research

Prioritize interview reports that are:

1. for the same or an adjacent technical role;
2. at comparable seniority;
3. within the last 24 months;
4. specific about stages, exercises, and expectations;
5. corroborated by other reports where possible.

Capture, when available:

- number and types of rounds;
- recruiter and hiring-manager stages;
- live-coding format;
- take-home assignments;
- system-design expectations;
- behavioral or executive interviews;
- candidate time burden;
- process consistency;
- communication quality;
- ghosting or unexplained delays;
- compensation changes during the process;
- candidate treatment;
- whether exercises resemble the actual work.

Do not infer a current interview process from one stale report.

# Interview Realism

Classify the available evidence as one of:

- `Realistic and role-aligned`
- `Demanding but proportionate`
- `Mixed or inconsistently reported`
- `Disproportionate or poorly aligned`
- `Potentially exploitative`
- `Insufficient evidence`

Evaluate realism independently from whether a reviewer passed, failed, withdrew, or received an offer.

Consider:

- relevance to actual responsibilities;
- seniority calibration;
- time burden;
- practicality of exercises;
- clarity of expectations;
- consistency across stages;
- respect for candidate time;
- whether the process tests the advertised job;
- whether take-home work resembles unpaid production work.

Use `Potentially exploitative` cautiously. It requires specific evidence of disproportionate uncompensated work, apparent production use, or comparable conduct; ordinary difficult interviewing is not enough.

# External Evidence Rules

## Source Tiers

### Higher-Confidence Sources

- official company sources;
- regulatory filings;
- investor or acquisition announcements;
- reputable journalism;
- direct leadership announcements;
- verified job postings.

### Contextual or Anecdotal Sources

- Glassdoor;
- Indeed;
- Blind;
- Comparably;
- Reddit;
- LeetCode Discuss;
- other employee-review or interview forums.

Use source triangulation where practical. Do not allow a review-platform score to replace the underlying themes, recency, role relevance, and sample limitations.

## Recency Labels

- `Current` — 0–12 months
- `Recent` — 13–24 months
- `Historical` — 25–48 months
- `Stale` — more than 48 months

Older evidence may establish history. It must not automatically be presented as a description of current conditions.

## Evidence Confidence

Use the following interpretation:

- `High` — current authoritative evidence plus multiple consistent, relevant reports;
- `Medium` — several relevant sources with meaningful but manageable limitations;
- `Low` — sparse, old, indirect, or conflicting evidence;
- `Insufficient` — evidence is not reliable enough to characterize the company or process.

State limitations directly.

# Artifact Separation

Opportunity recon is internal analysis. Company research, employee sentiment, Purple Squirrel scoring, pursuit economics, conversion-likelihood judgments, and interview-risk commentary must not appear in clean resumes, cover letters, recruiter responses, application answers, or follow-up messages unless the user explicitly requests an internal dossier or sendable language appropriate to that artifact.

# TruthGuard Integration

Opportunity recon must follow `rules/04-truthguard.md`.

In particular:

- do not convert anonymous reports into verified facts;
- do not generalize isolated allegations;
- do not represent historical evidence as current without corroboration;
- do not confuse similarly named organizations;
- do not interpret missing evidence positively or negatively;
- do not use candidate-market scarcity to erase candidate gaps;
- do not embed candidate facts, market statistics, or company facts as universal framework truth.
